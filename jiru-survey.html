Funerary Protected Area (Jiru) Survey in the Solomon Islands

Help us document jiru — Solomon Islands funerary protected areas

JiruTraditionally, jiru was applied to areas after the death of a prominent chief. From six months to one year, after the person died, the restriction would be lifted, and a large feast would be held to honour the deceased person (p. 354 of Woodley (2002) PhD Thesis) is a funerary protection practice observed in parts of the Solomon Islands. When someone in a community dies, certain marine areas (reefs, lagoons), freshwater resources (rivers, streams), or land areas (forests, gardens) may be placed under a customary taboo for a period of mourning. After the protection period ends, a communal harvest takes place — often as a feast honouring the deceased.

This indigenous conservation practice is deeply interwoven with cultural identity, resource management, and ecological resilience. However, jiru and similar funerary protected areas (FPAs) remain under-documented and are increasingly at risk as modern conservation programmes and socioeconomic changes reshape traditional governance systems.

This research builds on published work by indigenous Fijian scholar, Dr. Ron Vave, on funerary protected areas in Fiji. For more context, see this online news article, or detailed information in this journal articles on Fiji’s funerary protected areas, or their five (5) culturally protected water body practices.

Before you begin — please read this carefully

You are being invited to participate in a research study conducted by Dr. Ron Vave (University of Hawaiʻi at Mānoa) and Henry Kaniki (WWF Pacific). This study is approved by the University of Hawaiʻi Institutional Review Board (IRB).

Purpose: This study documents jiru and similar funerary resource protection practices across the Solomon Islands to better understand their distribution, cultural significance, and conservation value.

What participation involves: You will be asked questions about your knowledge of and experience with jiru practices in your community. The survey takes approximately 30 minutes to complete.

Voluntary participation: Your participation is entirely voluntary. You may skip any question you are not comfortable answering, and you may stop at any time without consequence.

Privacy and data use:

  • Your responses will be used for academic research purposes only.
  • Your name is collected solely to help map the distribution of jiru practices and for potential follow-up by the research team. This information will be held securely and will only be known to Dr. Vave and Henry Kaniki.
  • Your email address is optional. It is only needed if you are open to follow up where additional details are needed. It will never be used for any other purpose or shared without your explicit approval.
  • Community-level findings will (1) initially be shared anonymously through a publicly accessible dashboard following form submission, and (2) in a journal article. No individual will be identified in any public output without their explicit consent.
  • All data is stored securely by the Vave Lab at the University of Hawaiʻi at Mānoa.

Contact: If you have questions about this study, please contact:
Dr. Ron Vave — ronvave@hawaii.edu
Henry Kaniki — hkaniki@wwfpacific.org

For questions about your rights as a research participant, contact the UH Human Studies Program at uhirb@hawaii.edu or (808) 956-5007.
